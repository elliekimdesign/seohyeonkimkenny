Travelers are more likely to scan than to read, and to follow sightlines rather than floor plans.

I developed that perspective as a brand manager at Benefit Cosmetics across 15 domestic duty-free locations in Korea, working on 20+ SKU launches and promotions in airport and downtown stores.Previously

On store visits at Incheon Airport, I kept seeing the same pattern: passengers would walk in with limited time, take a quick lap, and let our visual cues guide them to what they needed. One mother and daughter who “still had about 40 minutes before boarding” scanned the space, tested just a couple of products, and went straight to the gift-set corner to check out within a few minutes.

INCHEON INTERNATIONAL AIRPORT · TERMINAL 1

As a brand manager, I used moments like this to adjust where we placed hero products, how we framed offers, and which signals we made most visible. As a design engineer now, I rely on the same instinct when I structure information architecture and user flows, focusing on what people see first, how they move through a journey, and how little friction it takes for them to decide.
